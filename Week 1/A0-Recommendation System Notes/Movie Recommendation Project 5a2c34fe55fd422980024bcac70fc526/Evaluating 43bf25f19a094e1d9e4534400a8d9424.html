Evaluating

Accuracy

Only for collaborative filtering, divide your date into train and test. Use your recommender to predict how much a user would rate the movie. Then, calculate

  • MAE
  • RMSE

Netflix created a competition for $1 million that used RMSE to evaluate recommender system using RMSE. Later, the winners’ model wasn’t used because Netflix realized RMSE wasn’t a good metric.

Hit Rate

  • Generate top-N recommendations for all users in test set
  • if one of the recommendations in a users’ top-N recommendations is something the user rated, consider that a ‘hit’
  • Add all ‘hits’ for every user and divide by number of users.
  • Little tricky because we are measuring for individuals users

Leave One Out Cross Validation

  • Compute top-N recommendations for each user in training data
  • Remove one of those items from users’ training data
  • Test our recommender system’s ability to recommend that item
  • Problem:
    • Hard to get one specific movie right while testing
    • Hit rate tends to be small

Hit Rate Measures

Average Reciprocal Hit Rate (ARHR)

  • similar to hit rate, but accounts for where in the top-N list your hits appear
  • more credit for successfully recommending an item in the top of the list
  • Instead of summing number of hit, we sum reciprocal rank of each hit.
  • If hit is on 3rd movie→ add up 1/3

Cumulative Hit Rate (CHR)

  • Throws away hits if our predicted rating is below some threshold.
  • Idea is we shouldn’t get credit for recommending items to a user that we think they won’t actually enjoy
  • If we had cutoff of 3 stars, we’d throw away the hits for items with less than 3 stars

Rating Hit Rate (RHR)

  • Break hit rate down into predicted rating score

Coverage

  • Percentage of possible recommendations that your system is able to provide
  • If we use MovieLens dataset which have several thousand movies generate recommendation on , IMDb, then the coverage of the system will be low.
  • If you enforce quality threshold on recommendations you make, then you might improve accuracy at the expense of coverage.

Diversity

  • how broad a variety of items your recommender puts in from of people
  • average similarity scores of all items in top-N recommendations and call it S, Similarity.
  • Diversity is the opposite of it.
  • D = 1 - S

Novelty

  • measure of how popular the items are that you are recommending

There’s a concept of user trust. People want to see some familiar items in their recommendations to make them believe that the recommendations they are getting are working.

  • need to strike a balance between popular and familiar
  • X axis all items and y axis is sales of particular item or popularity. Most of the sales come from small number of items. But the ‘long tail’ taken together accounts for a lot of sales. Those cater for different people with different needs.
  • Need to help people discover those items in the long tail that are relevant to them

Churn

  • how often do recommendations for a user change?
  • how sensitive your system is to new user behavior
  • showing the same recommendations too many times is a bad idea
  • randomizations in top-N can help

Responsiveness

  • how quickly does new user behavior influence the recommendations?
  • does it change instantly, or after a day?
  • need to strive balance between responsiveness and simplicity

A/B Testing

  • tune system using real customers; measure how they react to your recommendations
  • do they buy, watch, or otherwise indicate that they want it
