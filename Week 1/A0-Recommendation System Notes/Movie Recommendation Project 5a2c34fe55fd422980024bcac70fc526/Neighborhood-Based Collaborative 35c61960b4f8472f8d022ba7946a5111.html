Neighborhood-Based Collaborative

  • Find other people like you and recommend stuff that they like

Top-N Architecture

  • Initial architecture in Amazon for collaborative filtering
  • Start with data score of rating: implicit or explicit
  • Generate candidates to recommend by looking up other items similar to the items each user like
  • Rank those candidates, filter out stuff the user hasn’t already seen
  • Produce top-N recommendations

Heart of the problem is producing item-item or user-user similarity

Sparsity

  • One user may not have watched all the movies and not have rated all of them
  • This results in sparse data
  • Cosine sim cannot be used for sparse data
  • Computation Challenges:
    • Don’t want to waste time storing and processing all of the missing data
    • Use structures like “sparse arrays” that avoid storing all the empty space

Adjusted Cosine

  • Applicable mostly for measuring similarity between users based on their ratings.
  • Based on the idea that different people have different baselines
  • What Bob considers a 3-start movie may be different from what Alive considers a 3-star movie
  • Adjusted cosine attempts to normalize these differences
  • Replaces x and y with their variances → differences from the mean
  • Not so applicable for sparse data because the mean wont be able to represent the baseline of the user

Pearson Similarity

  • Instead of looking at the difference between ratings and a users’ average rating, we look at the difference between ratings and the average from all users for that given item.
  • Better with sparse data
  • Star Wars, a film most people like. People who hate Star Wars are going to get a strong similarity score from Pearson’s similarity, because they share opinions that are not mainstream

Spearman Rank Correlation

  • Pearson similarity based on ranks, not ratings

Mean Squared Difference

  • You look at all of the items that two user have in common and compute the mean of the squared differences between how each user rated each item
  • This calculates the difference between user, to get similarity, we take the inverse.
  • We add 1 in the denominator to precent divide by zero error

Jaccard Similarity

  • Imagine two users: A and B
  • The Jaccard sim between these two users is simply the size of the intersection between these two users’ ratings, divided by the union of those two users’ ratings.
  • Count all movies that both users indicated some sort of interest in, and divide by the total number of movies that either user indicated interest in.
  • Caveat here is that we are throwing explicit user rating. So, Jaccard is especially useful for implicit ratings.
